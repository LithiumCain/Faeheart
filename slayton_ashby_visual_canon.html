Canon Note — The Dragon Tax Slayton is the student whose dragon egg cracks during Banister's dueling chaos — a tiny, shimmering pink dragon imprints on him instantly and climbs onto his head like a crown. From that moment forward, he is a dragon-bonded student whether he was ready or not. The dragon follows him to class. The dragon hisses at Riven. The dragon never, ever gets off his head without negotiation.
Prose-Ready Description
First impression (Allura's POV) Tall, handsome, warm-smiled Slayton. Not wolf. Not owl. Not dragon-bonded — not yet. Something elegant and easy, someone whose magic felt like a window opened in a warm room. Soft brown hair he hasn't managed to style. Amber-hazel eyes that go earnest when he looks at her. He trips over the restaurant threshold on their first date and plays it off like it was on purpose. It almost works.
Neutral / third person shorthand He was the kind of boy who held doors and meant it. Soft-featured, easily flushed, entirely unguarded — and not who she was waiting for.
Slayton's narrative job is to be genuinely good — not a rival, not a villain, not a trick. He's what safe feels like when safe isn't the answer. When writing him, lean into warmth and small clumsiness. Let the reader almost root for him. That ache is the point.

Honey, bark, warm cream. Earth tones against a sea of cosmic midnight. He does not stand out. He is not meant to.

Energy
He escorts Allura and she says yes because he asked sweetly. He spends the evening trying not to stare. When Reign enters the ballroom with his dragons, the difference between the two men becomes one of the quietest, saddest things in the book.
The Breakup — Scene Canon Slayton's subplot ends with Allura choosing herself. She tells him, softly, that she can't do this. He begs — not manipulatively, just honestly. He knows, even as he asks, that she's waiting for someone. He's not angry when she walks away. He's relieved for her before he lets himself feel sad for himself. He is a good person. That's why the scene hurts.
The breakup scene needs to cost something. Slayton is not disposable — he's the first person at the Nexus who was kind to Allura with no agenda. When she leaves him, the reader should grieve for him, not cheer. The dragon on his head in the next scene is the comedy that lets us breathe.
Style Identity — The Through Line
Who Slayton dresses like He dresses like a boy who was taught to be kind, practical, and unflashy — and who hasn't had any reason to question any of it. Warm palette, soft silhouettes, nothing that asks you to look at him twice. He is not hiding. He just never learned to demand attention.
The role he was given Slayton is the boy Allura chose because he was safe. That is his entire narrative gift — and the reason she had to leave him. He is not a villain. He is a chapter. And he deserved to be written with care.
