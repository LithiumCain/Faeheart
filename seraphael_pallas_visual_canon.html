Canon Note — The Chipped Mug Her green tea lives in a chipped ceramic mug she refuses to replace. Students have offered new ones. Students have bought new ones. The mug remains. Nobody knows why, and she will not be explaining.
Prose-Ready Description
First impression (student POV) She does not raise her voice because she does not need to. Warm brown skin with faint ember-glow markings tracing her forearms. Dark hair pulled into a warrior's braid. Eyes sharp enough to correct your form from the back of the aerie. She smells like citrus and dragon-smoke. She drinks green tea from a chipped mug and breaks complex maneuvers into four clean steps that you will remember for the rest of your life.
Neutral / third person shorthand She was the kind of professor who expected greatness and made you believe you could reach it. Students adored her. Students also straightened their spines when she walked past.
Pallas is surgical. Her dialogue should be clean — short, precise, cutting without cruelty. She remembers birthdays but forgets her own. Her humor is dry enough to miss if you blink. Let students discover her warmth gradually.

Teaching Attire

Signature pieces
Ember-marked vambraces — her own dragon bond manifesting visibly. A silver pin at the collar shaped like a dragon's wing. Practical boots. The chipped mug.
What she avoids
Ceremony. Robes. Anything she can't move in. Anything that implies she has time to stand still.
Pallas's office has a tiny succulent garden on the windowsill — every plant named after an extinct dragon species. Students who learn the names are the ones she mentors personally. Nobody mentions this. Everyone notices.

Style Identity — The Through Line
Who Pallas dresses like She dresses like someone whose wardrobe answers to gravity, fire, and sudden takeoff. Every piece functional. Every detail earned. She is a warrior-scholar and her clothes know it. She does not need to perform authority — her clothes already did the work.
The teacher she is Warm, not soft. Approachable, not permissive. She expects effort, not perfection. Students who want to be told they're wonderful go elsewhere. Students who want to be taught find her and never leave.
